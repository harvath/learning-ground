LESSON 0007 · THE BETA SYSTEM

Inside TestFlight — how the beta system actually works

L0005 and L0006 stopped at "the build is uploaded." This lesson opens what happens on the other side of that upload — so a stuck build, a review rejection, or a tester who can't launch becomes diagnosable instead of mysterious.

Every earlier lesson pointed at TestFlight as the destination without looking inside it. Now we look inside. The key reframing: TestFlight is its own distribution channel that runs entirely on Apple's infrastructure, riding on the App Store provisioning profile from L0002. That's why your testers never register a device with you and never touch your certificate — once the build reaches App Store Connect, Apple re-signs it and delivers it. The trust chain L0002 described terminates at the upload; from there, the tester trusts the build because they trust Apple, not because they trust you.

Between "upload" and "testable"

A freshly uploaded build is not immediately installable. It passes through a fixed pipeline first, and each stage is a place a build can get stuck:

Processing
Apple validates the build (its provisioning profile, entitlements, required device capabilities) and thins it — splits your universal upload into per-device variants so each tester downloads only the slices their hardware needs.[1] Typically 5–30 minutes. Until it finishes, the build shows as Processing in the TestFlight tab and can't be assigned to anyone.
Export-compliance gate
Before a build can be distributed, Apple needs to know whether it uses non-exempt encryption. If your Info.plist doesn't already answer this, App Store Connect blocks the build behind a "Missing Compliance" prompt until you do (details below).[4]
Ready to test
Now the build can be added to a testing group. Internal groups get it immediately; external groups may first pass through Beta App Review (below).
the encryption question, concretely The gate is one Info.plist key: ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryption, a boolean.[4] Set it to NO and the "Missing Compliance" prompt never appears — this is the honest answer for most apps, since standard HTTPS/TLS and Apple's own crypto count as exempt. Leave the key out and App Store Connect asks you the same question by hand, on every build, before it'll let you distribute. It's not signing or review — it's a US export-regulation declaration Apple is legally required to collect. The reason it feels like it "randomly blocks" a build is simply that the key was never added to the plist, so the answer defaults to being asked interactively.

Two audiences, one build

L0005 gave you the internal-vs-external table as a shipping rule. Here's the mechanism underneath it. Both groups receive the same processed build; what differs is who qualifies, how many, and what stands between the build and them.

The dividing line is role membership. An "internal tester" is not a separate kind of account you create — it's an existing team member (someone already in your App Store Connect organization with one of those roles) whom you've flagged as a tester.[2] That's why internal testing needs no review: Apple already knows and trusts everyone in that list. External testers are, by definition, people Apple doesn't know in relation to your app — which is exactly why a review stands in front of them.

Beta App Review is not App Store review

When you add the first build of a version to an external group, Apple sends it to Beta App Review — a check that the build follows the App Review Guidelines before strangers can install it.[1] Two things make it much lighter than the full review a public release faces:

  • It's per version, not per build. The first build of 1.3 gets reviewed; build 2, 3, 4… of 1.3 usually go straight to testers with no wait, unless something review-relevant changed (new entitlements, new privacy strings). Bump to 1.4 and the next first build is reviewed again. This is why the build-number vs version-string distinction from L0005 matters here too: the version string is what resets the review requirement.
  • It's a lighter bar. Beta review confirms the build isn't broken or abusive and roughly follows the guidelines. Full App Store review — the future-mission bar — additionally scrutinizes metadata, screenshots, privacy disclosures, and business-model compliance. A build that sails through Beta App Review can still be rejected at real submission.
the trap this sets "It's been on TestFlight for weeks, so App Store approval is a formality" is the single most common wrong assumption here. Passing Beta App Review tells you almost nothing about whether the full review will pass — they check different things. Treat TestFlight approval as "testers can install," never as "the App Store will accept this."

How a tester actually gets the build

External testers get in one of two ways, and the difference is worth knowing because it changes who ends up on your 10,000-person list:[3]

  • Email invite — you enter specific addresses; each person gets a private invitation tied to that email.
  • Public link — a single URL anyone can tap to enrol themselves, up to a cap you set. This is how open betas work; the trade-off is you don't choose who joins, only how many.

Either way, the tester installs the free TestFlight app from the App Store, accepts the invite, and installs the thinned variant for their device.[1] New builds you push appear there automatically. And crucially, TestFlight is a genuine feedback channel, not just a delivery pipe: on recent TestFlight versions (iOS/macOS/visionOS), a tester can take a screenshot inside your beta app and annotate it as feedback, or send written feedback from the TestFlight app itself — and it arrives in App Store Connect attached to the exact build and device, alongside any crash reports.[5] Older tvOS/iOS clients fall back to emailing the feedback address you set in the build's test information.

Builds don't live forever

Every build expires 90 days after upload.[1] After that, testers see "Expired" and can't launch it — it isn't deleted, just made un-runnable, so a beta you shipped and forgot silently stops working for everyone on it around the three-month mark. You can also expire a build early by hand to pull it from testers before its 90 days are up (a bad build you want to retract immediately). A newer build assigned to the same group supersedes the old one for that group, but doesn't reset the older build's own expiry clock.

the Expo/EAS delta eas submit --platform ios uploads the build to App Store Connect using an App Store Connect API key (a token you register with Apple that lets a machine act on your behalf) instead of your interactive Xcode login.[6] That's the only thing that changes. Everything in this lesson — processing, the export-compliance gate, internal vs external, Beta App Review, groups, public links, feedback, 90-day expiry — is entirely on Apple's side of the upload and behaves identically no matter what put the build there. TestFlight has no idea whether a human or a CI job did the uploading.

Check it sticks

① A build finished uploading an hour ago but you still can't add it to your external tester group. Name the two most likely gates it's stuck behind.

Either it's still Processing (Apple validating and thinning it into per-device variants), or it's blocked on the export-compliance / "Missing Compliance" prompt because ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryption isn't set in the plist. If both are clear, an external group additionally has to clear Beta App Review on the first build of the version.

② You uploaded build 5 of version 1.3. Builds 1–4 of 1.3 already passed Beta App Review and reached external testers. What happens to build 5?

Beta App Review is required on the first build of each version. Later builds of that same version (1.3) typically skip it and reach testers immediately — unless something review-relevant (entitlements, privacy usage) changed. Bumping the version string to 1.4 is what would trigger review again.

③ Why does an external tester never have to register their device's UDID with you, the way a Development build requires?

This is L0002's App Store profile row made concrete. App Store / TestFlight distribution has no device list because Apple's own infrastructure re-signs the build for delivery; the device-level trust check happens on Apple's side. Device UDID lists only exist for Development and Ad Hoc profiles.

run it for real Open your app's TestFlight tab in App Store Connect and read the current build's state out loud in this lesson's vocabulary: is it Processing, is it flagged Missing Compliance, is it in an internal or external group, and how many days until it expires? Then check whether ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryption is set in your project — if it isn't, you've been answering that prompt by hand on every single upload, and setting it once ends that.
