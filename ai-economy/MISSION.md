# Mission: The Current AI Economy — Landscape, Terms, Money

## Why
The user wants broad, current competence in the AI industry as it stands right now — not to make one specific decision, but for three reasons at once: to evaluate and adopt AI tools competently as a builder, to reason sensibly about the business/economic side (which claims are durable vs. hype, who the real players are), and to follow the discourse (articles, podcasts, conversations) without embarrassing gaps. The field moves fast enough that knowledge decays in months, and the user explicitly does not want stale, half-remembered impressions passing for understanding — they want it grounded in real, dated research each time, and they want to know what used to be true but no longer is.

## Success looks like
- Can place any AI company named in an article into the right layer of the industry (foundation-model lab / infra-compute or "neocloud" / chipmaker / agent-tooling framework) and roughly state its current position
- Can explain and correctly use load-bearing 2025–2026 terms on first encounter: MCP, agent framework, RAG vs. long-context vs. fine-tuning, "ontology" (the 2026 enterprise/Palantir sense vs. the classical knowledge-representation sense), open-weight vs. open-source, vibe coding, AI-DLC
- Can read a funding/valuation headline critically — spot circular financing, distinguish revenue from burn, place the claim inside the live "is this a bubble" debate — rather than taking the number at face value
- Can name the current "big figures" (lab CEOs, notable recent departures/moves, prominent critics) and explain in one sentence why each is relevant right now
- Recognizes what's already dated (AutoGPT-style autonomous agents, "prompt engineer" as a job title, OpenAI Swarm, first-generation "wrapper" startups) so as not to reference a stale narrative as current
- Knows which 2–3 ongoing sources to keep checking after this course ends, since the course itself will start decaying the day it's written

## Constraints
- **Extremely fast-moving, transient topic.** Every lesson must be grounded in dated, cited real research — never parametric recall. Any moving figure (valuation, revenue, headcount, model name) carries an explicit "as of [date]" and a source.
- User is a software engineer (same person as the `ios-distribution` and other workspaces here) — technical material can move at a practitioner's pace, but the business/economics side should not assume finance background.
- Roughly **equal weight** between the technical ecosystem (frameworks, protocols, models) and the business/market layer (players, money, power) — treat it as one landscape, not two parallel tracks.
- Keep lessons short and tightly scoped — the domain's sheer breadth is the enemy here, not its difficulty. One lesson, one layer, one win.
- Content will go stale between sessions; expect (and welcome) periodic "what changed since we last talked" refresh lessons rather than treating this as a one-and-done course.

## Out of scope
- Deep ML/DL theory (transformer internals, training algorithms, optimizer math) beyond the minimum needed to understand a term in context
- Hands-on build tutorials for a specific framework (e.g., actually writing a Mastra agent) — a separate, build-focused course if the user wants one later
- Specific investment advice or stock-picking
