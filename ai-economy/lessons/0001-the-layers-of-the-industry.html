LESSON 0001 · THE LAYERS OF THE INDUSTRY

Labs, infra, and capital: the shape of the industry

Every AI headline names a company. Before any of those names mean anything, you need to know which of three layers it belongs to — because each layer plays a completely different game.

"AI company" hides three very different businesses under one label. A lab trains and sells access to models. An infrastructure company sells or rents the chips and data centers those models are trained and run on. A capital source — venture funds, hyperscaler balance sheets, sovereign wealth — supplies the money that makes the other two possible. Almost every confusing headline ("company X raises $30B," "company Y's stock drops 17% in a day") makes sense once you know which layer it's about and how money actually flows between the three.

The three layers

1. Labs — build and sell model access
OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta AI, xAI, Mistral, DeepSeek, and others. Their product is a model, sold via API/subscription (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) or given away as open weights. This is the layer that makes headlines about model releases and AGI claims (explained in the box just below) — but it's also the layer burning the most cash relative to revenue.
2. Infrastructure & compute — sell or rent the hardware
Chipmakers (Nvidia, Broadcom, AMD), cloud giants (Microsoft, Google, Amazon), and a newer category called neoclouds — GPU-specialized rental providers like CoreWeave, Nebius, and Lambda that exist purely to rent compute to labs that don't want to own hardware. This layer sells the shovels in the gold rush, and right now it's the layer actually printing profit, not just projecting it.
3. Capital — funds the other two
Venture funds (a16z, Sequoia), hyperscaler balance sheets (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta funding both their own labs and their cloud buildouts), and sovereign wealth (SoftBank, Gulf state funds). This layer's money mostly flows into labs and infrastructure, often as multi-year compute-purchase commitments rather than simple cash grants — which matters a lot for reading funding headlines correctly (more on this in L0003).
AGI — artificial general intelligence

The term for an AI system that matches or exceeds human performance across essentially the full range of cognitive tasks — not just narrow ones. Today's models are enormously capable at specific things (writing, coding, some kinds of reasoning), but "AGI" is a claim about generality, not raw capability. There is no agreed technical definition or benchmark for it — labs, researchers, and critics genuinely disagree about what would even count as reaching it, which is a big part of why it functions as much as a strategic and rhetorical term as a technical one.

In this economy specifically, AGI isn't just marketing language — it's written directly into contracts with real money attached. Amazon's stake from OpenAI's March 2026 round includes $35B contingent on an IPO/AGI-milestone condition, and Microsoft's technology-access rights to OpenAI's models are bounded through 2032 except for a specific carve-out covering any future model verified to be AGI.[9][10] Labs also use the term differently on purpose: OpenAI's stated mission centers on AGI explicitly, while Anthropic more often says "powerful AI" instead — partly to sidestep exactly this definitional fight.

The simplified flow: capital funds labs and infrastructure → labs rent compute from infrastructure providers, often via huge multi-year commitments → labs sell subscriptions/API access to consumers and businesses → that revenue is, for most labs today, nowhere near covering what they're spending on compute. Infrastructure providers get paid either way, which is why Nvidia and Broadcom are the industry's least contested profit centers right now, even while the labs layer runs enormous losses.[1]

Who's in each layer, right now

Labs. OpenAI — usage leader via ChatGPT, valued at $852B as of a round that closed in March 2026, but running roughly $14B in annual losses against ~$25B revenue.[2][3] Anthropic — now the higher-valued of the two ($965B, round closed ~May 2026), with meaningfully better unit economics: its burn is forecast to fall toward roughly 9% of revenue by 2027, versus OpenAI staying elevated.[4] Google DeepMind trails both on standalone valuation (folded into Alphabet) but leads on distribution — Gemini's usage share has grown fast simply because it's the default on Google's own products.[5] Meta AI, xAI, Mistral, and DeepSeek round out the field, each with a distinct strategy — Meta pivoting away from pure open-source, xAI now merged into SpaceX rather than standing alone, Mistral betting on European sovereignty, DeepSeek betting on cheap open-weight models.[6]

Infrastructure. Nvidia remains the dominant GPU supplier and the world's most valuable company by market cap.[1] But it's not unchallenged: Broadcom designs the custom chips (like Google's TPUs) that hyperscalers are increasingly building instead of buying more Nvidia GPUs — a quieter but very real erosion at the margin.[7] The neoclouds (CoreWeave, Nebius, Lambda) are worth knowing as a category even if you never need the individual names — they're the AI industry's landlords, existing purely to rent GPUs to labs.[8]

Check it sticks

① Name the three layers of the industry and, in one phrase each, what each one actually sells or supplies.

Labs (build and sell model access — API/subscription or open weights). Infrastructure & compute (sell or rent the chips and data centers). Capital (venture funds, hyperscaler balance sheets, sovereign wealth — funds the other two, often via compute-purchase commitments rather than plain cash).

② Why are Nvidia and Broadcom currently seen as less contested/risky than the labs layer, even though they're all "AI companies"?

They're in the infrastructure layer — they get paid for hardware/compute either way, and are posting real, current profits. The labs layer (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.) is running large losses relative to revenue, betting on future profitability that hasn't arrived yet.

③ Why does it matter, specifically for reading AI-economy headlines, that "AGI" has no agreed definition?

Because the term gets treated as a fixed technical milestone even though it isn't one — including inside real contracts (Amazon's $35B AGI-contingent tranche, Microsoft's post-2032 AGI carve-out from OpenAI). Without a fixed definition, "reaching AGI" is partly a negotiated, interpreted event rather than a clean fact you could just look up.

④ A neocloud like CoreWeave belongs to which layer?

Neoclouds exist specifically to rent GPU-heavy compute capacity to labs that don't want to buy and operate hardware themselves — squarely the infrastructure layer, distinct from the general-purpose hyperscaler clouds (AWS/Azure/GCP) and distinct from the labs that consume their capacity.
